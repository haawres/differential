THE VISION: FUTURE OF DRONES
THE CHALLENGE

Drones in the Modern Era

Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have transitioned from simple remote-controlled toys to highly sophisticated civilian assets. Today, they are transforming industries like livestock management—where they monitor grazing herds, survey terrain, and track animals across expansive fields.

However, true utility demands absolute autonomy. A drone cannot rely on constant manual telemetry or fragile GPS links in dense forests or remote fields. It must fly, perceive, decide, and stabilize its trajectory entirely on its own.

THE RELEVANCE

Why this Paper is Replicated

The paper we chose, Obstacle Avoidance-Based Autonomous Navigation of a Quadrotor System, is highly relevant because it directly bridges abstract mathematical theory with physical flight safety systems.

By mapping rigid-body Euler equations of motion, the authors model a 6-degree-of-freedom under-actuated system. They then implement a Genetic Algorithm to optimize altitude and attitude PID loops under two criteria: the highly-damped ITAE (Integral of Time-weighted Absolute Error) and the fast-response ISE (Integral of Square Error).

Finally, a LiDAR-based Bat Algorithm calculates steering vectors dynamically when obstacles are detected. This replication demonstrates how complex ordinary differential equations are transformed into a living, reactive autopilot.

Understanding the Paper

An overview of the quadrotor autonomous navigation model and optimization results.

Understanding the Paper

The research paper, Obstacle Avoidance-Based Autonomous Navigation of a Quadrotor System, presents the development of a mathematical model and simulation of a quadrotor capable of autonomous navigation while avoiding obstacles. The study is motivated by the growing use of quadrotors in livestock management, where they are used for monitoring, tracking, counting, and observing animals. Since autonomous operation requires the drone to move safely through its environment, the paper focuses on combining flight control with obstacle avoidance.

The paper models the quadrotor as a six-degree-of-freedom dynamic system using the Euler equations of motion. The mathematical model describes both the translational motion (movement along the x, y, and z axes) and the rotational motion (roll, pitch, and yaw). The quadrotor is treated as an under-actuated system with four control inputs generated by the four rotors and six output variables representing its position and orientation.

To control the quadrotor, the paper develops two Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controllers: one for altitude control and another for attitude control. The PID controller gains are optimized using a Genetic Algorithm (GA), with two objective functions—Integral Square Error (ISE) and Integral Time Absolute Error (ITAE). The performance of both tuning methods is compared using transient response measures such as overshoot, settling time, rise time, and steady-state error.

The complete navigation model includes four main components: the desired trajectory generator, the quadrotor dynamic model, the PID-based flight controllers, and an obstacle avoidance system. A LiDAR sensor is used to detect nearby obstacles. At the same time, an Elite Opposition-Based Bat Algorithm (EOBA) determines a new collision-free path whenever an obstacle is detected within a specified distance threshold. The quadrotor then follows the generated path through the flight controllers.

Simulation results show that the ITAE-tuned PID controller provides better overall control performance than the ISE-based controller, producing lower overshoot and smaller steady-state error. The obstacle avoidance system also enables the quadrotor to navigate successfully to its target while avoiding collisions. The study concludes that a distance threshold of 1 m is sufficient to avoid obstacles with a width of up to 1 m, making the proposed navigation system suitable for autonomous outdoor applications such as livestock monitoring.

Rigid Body Formulation

The paper details the autonomous navigation of a quadrotor Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV). A quadcopter is a rotary-wing system driven by 4 independent actuators arranged in a '+' configuration. The thrust force generated by each rotor (fi) and their reactive drag torques (τi) are proportional to the square of the rotor angular speed (ωi):

Actuator Speed Mapping
fi = b · ωi2 ,      τi = d · ωi2
b : Thrust factor coefficient (6.317 × 10-4 N · s2 / rad2)
d : Drag factor coefficient (1.610 × 10-4 N · m · s2 / rad2)

Three-Step Autonomy Pipeline

Autonomous navigation relies on a closed-loop system dividing tasks into three main categories:

👁

1. Perception

Combines GPS and IMU sensors for internal state estimation, and a 2D LiDAR sensor for obstacle mapping within a 7m range.

📍

2. Planning

Calculates goal-seeking vectors and executes an Elite Opposition-Based Bat Algorithm when obstacles enter the safety clearance threshold.

⚙

3. Control

Toggles PID feedback loops to calculate the necessary rotor speeds (ω1, ω2, ω3, ω4) to follow planned vectors.

Ordinary Differential Equations of Motion

Translational kinematics and rotational dynamics derived through the Newton-Euler formalism.

Translational Kinematics (x, y, z accelerations)

The translational acceleration of the quadrotor in the earth-fixed frame is driven by the total thrust (U1) rotated through roll (φ), pitch (θ) and yaw (ψ) commands:

Translational Kinematics ODEs
x¨ = (U1 / m) · (cosφ sinθ cosψ + sinφ sinψ)

y¨ = (U1 / m) · (cosφ sinθ sinψ - sinφ cosψ)

z¨ = (U1 / m) · (cosφ cosθ) - g
U1: Total vertical thrust generated by all 4 motors.
m: Mass of the quadrotor (1.888 kg).
g: Gravity acceleration (9.81 m/s2).

Rotational Dynamics (Roll, Pitch, Yaw moments)

The rotational acceleration equations describe angular acceleration under applied torques (U2, U3, U4) and gyroscopic motor coupling factors:

Rotational Kinematics ODEs
φ¨ = (U2 · l / Ix) - [ (Iz - Iy) / Ix ] · θ• ψ• - (Jr ωr / Ix) · θ•

θ¨ = (U3 · l / Iy) - [ (Ix - Iz) / Iy ] · φ• ψ• + (Jr ωr / Iy) · φ•

ψ¨ = (U4 / Iz) - [ (Iy - Ix) / Iz ] · φ• θ•
U2, U3, U4: Torques corresponding to rolling, pitching, and yawing motion.
ωr: Residual motor angular velocity: ω1 - ω2 + ω3 - ω4.
Jr: Rotor moment of inertia (2.82 × 10-7 kg · m2).
Ix, Iy, Iz: Moments of inertia along the coordinate axes.

Simulator Physics Details

  • ITAE vs. ISE: Selecting ITAE configures a highly-damped PID loop. Selecting ISE sets higher gains with low damping, causing overshoot and trajectory tracking oscillations (as shown in Figure 13).
  • 1.0m vs. 0.5m Threshold: A 0.5m distance threshold is smaller than the physical radius clearance of the drone and obstacle, causing collision. A 1.0m threshold allows the Bat Algorithm steering vectors to bank the drone away.
  • Sensory Lag: Artifically delays state estimation vectors in the feedback loop. High lag values trigger unstable oscillations, demonstrating the impact of transport delay on differential equation stability.

Controller Tuning Optimization

The genetic algorithm optimized the PID gains (Kp, Ki, Kd) by minimizing two distinct error indices. The Integral of Time-weighted Absolute Error (ITAE) heavily penalizes errors that persist over time, while the Integral of Square Error (ISE) penalizes large initial transient errors.

Optimization Cost Indices
ISE = ∫0tsim e2(t) dt                  ITAE = ∫0tsim t · |e(t)| dt

Future Improvements & Research Areas

Scaling models to support multi-agent swarms, optical-flow integration, and alternative tuning routines.

🤝

Swarm Autonomy

Scaling the mathematical model from a single quadcopter to collaborative multi-agent swarms. This requires adding mutual collision avoidance constraints and sharing localized LiDAR maps to coordinate navigation vectors.

👁

GPS-Denied Vision

Integrating computer vision and optical flow systems. Using neural networks and optical flow sensors to estimate state vectors when GPS coordinates are unavailable or blocked by tree coverage in livestock environments.

⚙

Alternative Tuning

Comparing the Genetic Algorithm optimization parameters against alternative metaheuristic tuning mechanisms, such as Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Cuckoo Search, and Smell Agent Optimization (SAO).
