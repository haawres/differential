### Our Mission

To practically apply the knowledge we have gained of differential equations to improve an existing model 

Most current climate research using ordinary differential equations (ODEs) focuses almost entirely on modeling physical systems&mdash;like predicting temperature surges from emissions, rising sea levels from ice melt, or biodiversity loss from deforestation.

These models are powerful diagnostic tools, but they largely ignore a crucial use case: testing how real-world policy interventions might alter those trajectories. We can simulate the damage of unchecked emissions&mdash;but rarely do these models explore how tools like carbon taxes or stricter land-use laws could actually mitigate that damage.

This gap is surprising, especially since ODEs are inherently flexible&mdash;parameters like emission rates, deforestation speed, or coastal degradation can be modified to reflect specific policy scenarios. Yet few studies leverage this to simulate and compare the outcomes of different strategies.

By focusing solely on physical predictions, these models miss their potential to inform decisions&mdash;like setting effective carbon pricing or balancing environmental rules with economic needs. It&rsquo;s time we shift ODEs from just forecasting climate risks to helping design smart, actionable climate solutions.

### DETAILED ANALYSIS

The mathematical model in the paper, &ldquo;A Mathematical Model to Investigate the Frequent Impact of Global Warming on Coastal Lives&rdquo; details the effects of greenhouse gas emissions and climate change on human populations in coastal areas.

Human activities release greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, which consequently increase global temperatures. High concentrations of the greenhouse gases and climate change have a negative effect on human population and forest ecosystem.

The paper models the four factors involved: humans, greenhouse gases, atmospheric temperature, and forest ecosystem as four different compartments. Each interaction between any two compartments is affected by a certain rate.

Designed for policymakers and researchers, it serves as both an early-warning system for climate tipping points and a testbed for mitigation strategies. Click below to view the equations.

### MODIFICATIONS

The climate change model provides a clear interaction between humans, forest ecosystems, Greenhouse gases and atmospheric temperature. However, more could have been done to improve the model.

Readjusting &delta;2 parameter : In the paper, the writers provided a rate of 0.0029 for GHG emissions by animals while humans were given a rate of 0.0011. From research and data analysis, animal contributions to GHG emissions is just 14% while humans take the vast majority. 

Introducing climate change policy factor, k: While the paper provides a good estimate of the causes of climate change, a term for the effects of climate change policies is not included. Climate change policies reduce GHG emissions, so the term k is subtracted from the GHG stock. This helps provide a more realistic measure of the GHG emission.

### Parameter Sensitivity

​The original model depicts a dire trajectory where greenhouse gases (GHGs) surge exponentially due to high human emissions (&delta;₁ = 0.011) and inadequate forest absorption (&delta;₃ = 0.050), driving temperature increases that trigger catastrophic feedback loops. By year 100, GHGs exceed 0.8 normalised units, temperatures spike by 125% from baseline, and these dual pressures collapse both human populations (declining 40% from peak) and forests (plummeting to 10% coverage).In contrast, the modified scenario demonstrates how strategic interventions&mdash;reducing animal-based emissions (&delta;₂ lowered from 0.029 to 0.004), boosting forest CO₂ uptake (&delta;₃ increased to 0.060), and curbing deforestation (&epsilon;₂ halved to 0.048)&mdash;alter this trajectory.

GHGs now plateau at 0.5 units, temperature rise slows by 33%, and both human and forest systems stabilize near 50% of their original capacities. These quantitative differences validate that preemptive policy changes can disrupt climate feedback loops before they reach tipping points. The comparison highlights how policy-driven adjustments alter system dynamics: where the original model predicts irreversible damage, the modified version achieves equilibrium. Lower emissions and stronger forest absorption slow GHG accumulation, while reduced deforestation and temperature sensitivity allow ecosystems to rebound.

Human populations stabilize as health impacts from pollution and warming diminish. This aligns with the paper&rsquo;s conclusion that reforestation and emission cuts are critical for sustainability. The graphs provide quantitative proof that climate policies can rewrite a dire trajectory into a manageable one.

### FURTHER RESEARCH

Spatial-Temporal Modeling with Partial Differential Equations (PDEs)

The current model treats coastal regions as uniform, ignoring local variations in elevation, land use, and ocean proximity. PDEs could capture how disasters like flooding propagate inland or how urban heat islands intensify warming. This would allow for targeted interventions in high-risk zones, such as prioritizing mangrove restoration in low-lying areas.

Modeling Environmental Policies and Their Impact

Policies like carbon taxes or renewable subsidies are often oversimplified in climate models. Explicitly integrating policy mechanisms could reveal how specific regulations slow GHG accumulation or reduce temperature rise. For instance, simulating a carbon tax could show how revenue reinvestment in coastal defenses mitigates disaster frequency.

Extending the Model to Non-Coastal Terrains

While the focus is on coastal vulnerability, expanding the framework to deserts, forests, or polar regions would broaden its applicability. For example, permafrost thaw in the Arctic releases methane, and droughts in arid regions disrupt agriculture&mdash;phenomena requiring distinct parameterizations but similar dynamical structures.

Incorporating a Green Tax Mechanism

A green tax could be modeled as a feedback loop where higher emissions incur greater penalties, forcing industries to adopt cleaner practices. This would directly reduce the GHG growth rate in the equations, with revenue optionally funding reforestation or seawall projects. Real-world carbon pricing data could calibrate its effectiveness.
